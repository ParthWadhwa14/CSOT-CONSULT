# Week 4: Recommendations & Executive Communication

**Total estimated time:** ~8–10 hours across 5 days. In Weeks 1–3, you learned how consultants structure business problems, analyze industries, gather evidence, and generate insights. This week focuses on transforming analysis into recommendations. You will learn how consultants evaluate alternatives, build persuasive arguments, structure their thinking, and communicate recommendations clearly to decision-makers. Effective recommendations must still be tested, refined, and ultimately translated into action.

---

## Day 1: Prioritization & Decision Making
**Estimated time:** ~1.5 hours

Consultants are expected to determine which option should be pursued and why. In most business situations, resources are limited, trade-offs are unavoidable, and multiple alternatives may appear attractive. Day 1 introduces the frameworks consultants use to evaluate alternatives, compare trade-offs, and prioritize actions.

### Read first:
1. **"RAPID Decision-Making: Making Good Decisions Quickly"**
   * **Source:** Bain & Company
   * **Overview:** Introduces Bain's RAPID framework, a structured approach used by organizations to improve the quality and speed of decision-making. Explains the different decision roles—Recommend, Agree, Perform, Input, and Decide—and how defining responsibilities avoids delays, confusion, and poor execution.
   * **Link:** https://www.bain.com/insights/rapid-decision-making/
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

2. **"An Introduction to the Weighted Decision Matrix"**
   * **Source:** Lucid
   * **Overview:** Explains how complex decisions can be broken into evaluation criteria and compared systematically using a weighted decision matrix. Demonstrates how decision-makers assign importance to criteria, score alternatives, and calculate rankings.
   * **Link:** https://lucid.co/blog/weighted-decision-matrix
   * **Estimated time:** 10 minutes

### Then watch:
* **Video 1: "How to Prioritize Like a Consultant"**
  * **Source:** Firm Learning (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** A practical walkthrough of how consultants evaluate competing opportunities and decide where to focus their efforts. Focuses on the distinction between generating options and selecting the most valuable option based on evidence and business objectives.
  * **Estimated time:** 10–15 minutes

---

> ### Day 1 Reflection Question
> Imagine Sarvam AI's leadership team has narrowed its future strategy to three options: becoming primarily a sovereign AI provider for governments, focusing on enterprise voice agents for businesses, or building consumer-facing AI products. What criteria would you use to evaluate these options? Which criteria should matter most: market size, profitability, competitive advantage, speed to scale, or something else? How might different stakeholders—investors, founders, customers, and government partners—prioritize these criteria differently?

## Day 2: Developing Strategic Recommendations
**Estimated time:** ~2 hours

Identifying an interesting insight is not the same as making a recommendation. Consultants are hired not only to analyze a situation, but to advise clients on what they should do next. Strong recommendations are specific, actionable, supported by evidence, and aligned with a client's objectives and constraints. Day 2 focuses on how consultants move from analysis to action. You will learn how recommendations are developed, evaluated, and refined before being presented to decision-makers.

### Read first:
3. **"What Makes a Good Strategy?"**
   * **Source:** Harvard Business Review
   * **Overview:** A classic article that explains the difference between a genuine strategy and a collection of goals, initiatives, or aspirations. Focuses on understanding why effective recommendations require making choices and accepting trade-offs rather than pursuing every opportunity simultaneously.
   * **Link:** https://hbr.org/2011/05/what-is-strategy
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

4. **"Strategy Is About Making Choices"**
   * **Source:** Harvard Business School Online
   * **Overview:** Explains why successful organizations focus on a limited set of priorities rather than attempting to maximize every objective. Pay attention to the role of trade-offs and how strategic recommendations often require deciding what not to do.
   * **Link:** https://online.hbs.edu/blog/post/what-is-strategy
   * **Estimated time:** 10–15 minutes

### Then watch:
* **Video 2: "What Is Strategy? It’s a Lot Simpler Than You Think"**
  * **Source:** Harvard Business Review (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** A concise explanation of strategy and strategic decision-making. Focuses on how organizations create competitive advantage by making deliberate choices rather than simply reacting to opportunities as they arise.
  * **Estimated time:** 10 minutes

---

> ### Day 2 Reflection Question
> Assume Sarvam AI decides that enterprise voice agents represent its most attractive opportunity. What specific recommendation would you make to the leadership team? Simply saying “focus on enterprise” is not enough. Consider questions such as: Which industries should Sarvam target first? What capabilities should it invest in? What businesses or initiatives should it deprioritize? What risks would accompany this strategy, and how would you address them? Try to make your recommendation specific enough that a leadership team could act on it.

## Day 3: Storylining & The Pyramid Principle
**Estimated time:** ~2 hours

Consultants often discover that having a strong recommendation is only half the challenge. The other half is communicating it clearly. Senior executives rarely have the time or patience to work through every piece of analysis in the order it was conducted. Instead, they want to know the answer first, followed by the reasoning that supports it. Day 3 introduces one of the most influential communication frameworks in consulting: the Pyramid Principle. You will learn how consultants structure arguments, organize evidence, and create narratives that are easy for decision-makers to follow.

### Read first:
5. **"The Pyramid Principle"**
   * **Source:** MindManager
   * **Overview:** Introduces the core concepts behind Barbara Minto’s Pyramid Principle, a framework used extensively in consulting and business communication. Focuses on understanding why recommendations should be presented first, with supporting arguments and evidence organized beneath them.
   * **Link:** https://www.mindmanager.com/en/blog/pyramid-principle/
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

6. **"Why Structured Communication Matters"**
   * **Source:** Management Consulted
   * **Overview:** Explains how consultants organize their thinking before creating presentations or reports. Pay attention to how arguments are broken into logical supporting points and why clarity often matters more than the amount of information presented.
   * **Link:** https://managementconsulted.com/consulting-presentation/
   * **Estimated time:** 10–15 minutes

### Then watch:
* **Video 3: "The Pyramid Principle Explained"**
  * **Source:** YouTube
  * **Overview:** A visual explanation of the Pyramid Principle and answer-first communication. Focuses on how complex recommendations can be broken into a hierarchy of supporting arguments, making them easier for executives to understand and evaluate.
  * **Estimated time:** 10–15 minutes

---

> ### Day 3 Reflection Question
> Imagine you have completed your analysis and concluded that Sarvam AI should focus primarily on enterprise voice agents rather than consumer products or government contracts. How would you communicate this recommendation to a CEO in less than two minutes? What would your opening recommendation be? What three supporting arguments would you present first? Which pieces of evidence would strengthen each argument? Consider how your communication would differ if you were speaking to an investor, a government official, or Sarvam’s leadership team.

## Day 4: Executive Communication & Slide Design
**Estimated time:** ~2 hours

Consultants rarely communicate their findings through long reports. Instead, recommendations are typically delivered through presentations, executive summaries, and concise discussions with decision-makers. The challenge is not simply presenting information, but presenting the right information in a way that allows executives to quickly understand the recommendation and its implications. Day 4 focuses on how consultants communicate recommendations effectively through slides, visualizations, and executive-level messaging.

### Read first:
7. **"The Basic Rules of Slide Design"**
   * **Source:** Duarte
   * **Overview:** Introduces the principles of effective slide design, including clarity, simplicity, visual hierarchy, and storytelling. Focuses on how slides should support a recommendation rather than overwhelm the audience with information.
   * **Link:** https://www.duarte.com/presentation-skills-resources/
   * **Estimated time:** 10–15 minutes

8. **"How to Write an Executive Summary"**
   * **Source:** Harvard Business Review
   * **Overview:** Explains how leaders consume information and why concise summaries are often more important than detailed reports. Pay attention to how recommendations, supporting arguments, and implications are communicated in a small amount of space.
   * **Link:** https://hbr.org/2016/11/how-to-write-an-executive-summary
   * **Estimated time:** 10 minutes

### Then watch:
* **Video 4: "Consulting Slide Design: What Makes a Great Slide?"**
  * **Source:** Firm Learning (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** A practical walkthrough of how consultants structure slides, write slide titles, and present evidence. Focus on the idea that every slide should communicate a single key message and that slide titles should often contain conclusions rather than topics.
  * **Estimated time:** 10–15 minutes

---

> ### Day 4 Reflection Question
> Imagine you have been given only three slides to persuade Sarvam AI’s board of directors to adopt your recommended business model. What would each slide contain? What would the title of each slide be? What evidence would you include, and what information would you deliberately leave out? Consider how your presentation would differ if your audience consisted of investors, customers, or government stakeholders instead of the board.

## Day 5: Bringing Everything Together
**Estimated time:** ~1.5 hours

Throughout the past four weeks, you have learned how consultants structure problems, analyze markets, gather evidence, develop insights, evaluate alternatives, and communicate recommendations. A common misconception among beginners is that consulting is a collection of independent frameworks. In reality, consulting is about connecting these tools into a coherent decision-making process. Day 5 is about stepping back and integrating everything you have learned before preparing your final recommendation for Sarvam AI.

### Read:
9. **"The Consultant's Toolkit: From Problem Solving to Recommendations"**
   * **Source:** Management Consulted
   * **Overview:** A concise overview of how consulting projects move from problem definition to analysis, insight generation, and final recommendations. Focus on how each stage builds on the previous one and why strong recommendations require both rigorous analysis and clear communication.
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

10. **"The Pyramid Principle and Consulting Communication"**
    * **Source:** StrategyU
    * **Overview:** Revisits the idea of answer-first communication and explains how consultants connect evidence, arguments, and recommendations into a persuasive storyline. Read this as a recap of the week's content rather than a new concept.
    * **Estimated time:** 10–15 minutes

### Then watch:
* **Video 5: "How Consultants Present Recommendations to Clients"**
  * **Source:** Management Consulted (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** A practical discussion of how consultants communicate final recommendations to executives and decision-makers. Focus on how recommendations are justified, how objections are addressed, and how evidence is used to support key arguments.
  * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

---

> ### Day 5 Reflection Question
> Imagine you are presenting your final recommendation to Sarvam AI’s founders. You have five minutes to explain which business model the company should prioritize over the next three to five years. How would you structure your recommendation? What evidence from Weeks 2 and 3 would you rely on most heavily? Which alternative strategies would you reject, and why? What risks could undermine your recommendation, and how would you address them if challenged by the leadership team? Try to think not only about what recommendation you would make, but also about how you would persuade others that it is the right decision.

## Task Week 4 Deliverable (submitted for evaluation)

Using the Sarvam AI problem statement and the tools from this week, produce a **one-page recommendation outline**. This is your Week 4 assessed submission.

### Your submission must include, on a single page with no prose paragraphs:
* **One clearly stated recommendation:** A specific claim about which business model Sarvam should prioritize.
* **3 supporting arguments:** Each argument should support your recommendation and be backed by evidence gathered during Weeks 2 and 3.
* **One recommendation pyramid:** A top-down diagram or nested bullet outline mapping: `Recommendation` $\rightarrow$ `Supporting Arguments` $\rightarrow$ `Evidence`.
* **One key uncertainty:** A specific question or assumption that could change your recommendation if new information became available.
* **One stakeholder perspective:** Choose one stakeholder group (founders, investors, enterprise customers, developers, or government partners) and explain how they would evaluate your recommendation.
* **One executive summary:** Maximum **75 words** summarizing your recommendation and rationale.

### Before submitting, check your outline against these questions:
* Have you clearly distinguished between evidence, arguments, and recommendations?
* Does each supporting argument directly support your recommendation?
* Have you identified a meaningful uncertainty rather than assuming your recommendation is unquestionably correct?
* Could a stakeholder reasonably disagree with your recommendation while accepting your evidence?
* Does your executive summary communicate both the recommendation and the reasoning behind it?
* Could new information realistically change your conclusion?

**Format:** 1 page (clarity of logic matters more than visual polish). Submit within Week 4.
