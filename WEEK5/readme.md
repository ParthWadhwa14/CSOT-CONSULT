**Total estimated time:** ~8–10 hours across 5 days. Concepts build on each other; do them in order.

## Day 1: The Argument Problem: Why Most Slides Fail
**Estimated time:** ~1.5 hours

By Week 5, you have done the analytical work. You have sized the market, stress-tested a financial model, applied strategic frameworks, and arrived at a view on what Sarvam should do. Now comes the part that determines whether any of that work reaches the panel.

Most slides fail not because the analysis is wrong but because the analyst never makes the shift from organising information to making an argument. A deck structured around topics (markets, competitors, financials) gives the audience material. A deck structured around a recommendation, with every slide providing a reason to believe it, makes a case. The distinction is not cosmetic: one asks the audience to form their own conclusion; the other earns their agreement before they leave the room. Day 1 establishes what that shift looks like before introducing any specific tool.

   * **Overview:** Explains how consulting engagements move between two modes: bottom-up analysis (gathering data, testing hypotheses) and top-down communication (starting with the conclusion, supporting it with evidence). Most people know only the first mode. This week is about the second. Read it as context for everything that follows.
   * **Link:** https://strategyu.co/how-scqa-and-the-pyramid-principle-fit-into-the-strategy-consulting-process/

Read this before watching today's video.

## Day 2: The Pyramid Principle
**Estimated time:** ~2 hours

The Pyramid Principle is the most important communication framework in management consulting. Developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey in the 1970s, it remains the standard for structuring written and verbal communication across every major firm. Day 2 covers the framework in full: the logic behind it, how to build with it, and the companion framework (SCQA) that governs how you open any deck.

### Read first:
3. **"Structure Your Ideas: Pyramid Principle Part 1"**
   * **Source:** StrategyU
   * **Overview:** Covers the synthesis half of the Pyramid Principle, working bottom-up from raw data to structured argument. You have spent four weeks gathering evidence on Sarvam. This article addresses the concrete question of how to move from a pile of findings to a coherent, hierarchical point of view. The worked example is clear, and the MECE requirement at each level connects directly to the Week 1 material on issue trees.
   * **Link:** https://strategyu.co/structure-your-ideas-pyramid-principle-part-1/
   * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

4. **"Pyramid Principle Part 2: Communicate Top-Down"**
   * **Source:** StrategyU
   * **Overview:** Covers the communication half: flipping the synthesis you did in Part 1 and leading with the conclusion. The critical section for Week 5 is the comparison between direct storytelling (answer first) and indirect storytelling (build to the answer). For the Sarvam pitch, you will need to decide which the panel warrants.
   * **Link:** https://strategyu.co/pyramid-principle-part-2-communicate-top-down/
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

### Then watch:
* **Video 2: "The Minto Pyramid Principle Explained with Examples"**
  * **Source:** EPM (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** An 8-minute visual walkthrough of the full pyramid structure from first principles. Covers the three tiers (governing thought, supporting arguments, evidence), the MECE requirement at each level, and how the same logic applies whether you are writing an email or building a 40-slide deck.
  * **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j4Y3TdVVBCA
  * **Estimated time:** 8 minutes

* **Video 3: "SCQA: Situation, Complication, Question, Answer, A Consulting Problem Solving Framework"**
  * **Source:** StrategyU (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** Covers the SCQA framework in detail, using a university budget scenario to show how each element works. The most important segment is the distinction between the Situation (shared, agreed-upon context) and the Complication (the specific change or tension that makes action necessary now). A Complication that reads like background rather than a reason to act loses the panel immediately.
  * **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0JVV1H_uD_M
  * **Estimated time:** 8 minutes

---

> ### Day 2 Exercise
> Write the SCQA opening for your Sarvam recommendation in four sentences, one per element:
> * **Situation:** *(shared context; something a panel member would know and agree with)*
> * **Complication:** *(what has changed or is at stake; specific to Sarvam, right now)*
> * **Question:** *(what naturally arises from the complication; the decision the deck answers)*
> * **Answer:** *(your recommendation, stated directly)*
> 
> *Do not reference charts, models, or evidence in the SCQA itself. The SCQA is the frame that creates the appetite for your evidence; it is not a preview of it. Read your SCQA aloud to someone unfamiliar with Sarvam. Can they tell you, without prompting, what the problem is and what you think should be done? If not, revise.*

## Day 3: Deck Architecture
**Estimated time:** ~2 hours

A pyramid gives you the logic. A deck is the order in which you reveal that logic to an audience. The two are not identical. Day 3 is about the architecture of a full consulting deck: how slides are grouped into sections, what the executive summary actually does, and the concrete process that practitioners use to build a deck from the argument outward rather than from blank slides inward.

### Read first:
5. **"How McKinsey Consultants Make Presentations"**
   * **Source:** Slideworks
   * **Overview:** A section-by-section breakdown of the standard consulting deck structure: title, executive summary, context, analysis, recommendation, next steps. The most practically useful section explains what the executive summary must do: it is not a table of contents or a preview. It is a standalone argument that a time-pressed executive could act on without reading the rest of the deck.
   * **Link:** https://slideworks.io/resources/how-mckinsey-consultants-make-presentations
   * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

6. **"PowerPoint for Consultants: Tips and Tricks"**
   * **Source:** PrepLounge
   * **Overview:** Written from the perspective of working consultants on what the slide-building process actually looks like on a real engagement. The most useful section describes the "dot-dash" or storyline process: before opening PowerPoint, write the argument as 10–20 bullets, each one the seed of a slide title. When read as a paragraph, they should tell the complete story.
   * **Link:** https://www.preplounge.com/en/blog/consulting/career/powerpoint-skills
   * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

### Then watch:
* **Video 4: "Consulting Presentation Tips from Former McKinsey Consultant"**
  * **Source:** Firm Learning (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** Covers the structural decisions that define a well-built consulting deck: how to sequence sections, what an executive summary slide should contain, and how to keep the overall argument tight across 10–15 slides. Shows the specific choices a practitioner makes when building a presentation.
  * **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YXQWfTPQoSg
  * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

## Day 4: Slide-Level Design: One Message Per Slide
**Estimated time:** ~2 hours

Architecture tells you what goes in the deck. Slide design tells you how each slide earns its place. The single most important principle is that every slide carries exactly one message, stated explicitly as a complete-sentence headline. Everything else on the slide exists to prove that headline. Day 4 covers what this means in practice and how to choose the visual that makes the proof obvious.

### Read first:
7. **"How to Write Slide Action Titles Like McKinsey (With Examples)"**
   * **Source:** Slideworks
   * **Overview:** Draws the line between a topic title ("Revenue Trends," "Competitive Landscape") and an assertion headline ("Revenue has declined across all segments, driven by volume loss in Tier 2 cities"). Topic titles label; assertion headlines argue. The article includes worked before-and-after rewrites and the recommended four-step process.
   * **Link:** https://slideworks.io/resources/how-to-write-action-titles-like-mckinsey
   * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

8. **"Consulting Slide Standards: Rules McKinsey, BCG, and Bain Follow"**
   * **Source:** Deckary
   * **Overview:** A practitioner's account of the specific design rules that separate client-ready consulting slides from near-misses. Covers the action title requirement, the one-message-per-slide rule, source citation conventions, and the titles test (if a senior executive reads only the slide titles in sequence, they should understand the full argument).
   * **Link:** https://deckary.com/blog/consulting-slide-standards
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

9. **"Think Before You Chart: Why McKinsey Bans Pie Charts"**
   * **Source:** StrategyU
   * **Overview:** The consulting approach to data visualisation: decide the message first, then choose the chart that proves it most obviously. Covers the rule that 80–90% of consulting charts are bar, column, or line charts and explains why.
   * **Link:** https://strategyu.co/think-before-you-chart/
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

> ### Day 4 Exercise
> Take the five most important slides in your Sarvam deck from your Day 3 outline and write an assertion headline for each. Apply three tests:
> 1. Does this headline make a specific claim, or does it describe a topic?
> 2. If someone read only this headline and nothing else, would they know exactly what to believe?
> 3. Is the claim specific enough to be wrong? *"Sarvam faces competitive pressure"* cannot be wrong. *"Sarvam's API business faces margin erosion within 18 months as Azure and Google bundle Indian-language capabilities into existing enterprise contracts"* can be wrong, and therefore is an argument, not a label.

## Day 5: Applying the Week to the Sarvam Pitch
**Estimated time:** ~1.5 hours

No new frameworks today. Your tools are the Pyramid Principle, SCQA, deck architecture, assertion headlines, and visual type logic. Day 5 converts them into the storyboard that is your Week 5 submission and your brief for Week 6.

    * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

### Guided warm-up (not submitted, 20 minutes):
Before building your storyboard, write answers to these three questions:
1. **What is the single sentence your audience should leave the room believing?** Write it exactly; this becomes the headline of your executive summary and the anchor of your recommendation slide.
2. **What are the three things a panel member would most want to push back on?** For each, identify which slide in your deck addresses it with evidence. If any of the three has no corresponding slide, you have a structural gap.
3. **What is your strongest alternative to your recommendation, and where in the deck do you acknowledge and defeat it?** If the deck never engages the strongest counter-argument, a panel will raise it on their own terms.

## Week 5 Deliverable: Pitch Deck Storyboard

This is the skeleton of the deck you will present in Week 6. Visual polish is not evaluated. What is evaluated is narrative logic: whether your slides, read in sequence, make a coherent argument that leads clearly to your recommendation.

### Your submission must include:
* **A written SCQA opening:** Four sentences, one per element, framing the Sarvam problem and your recommendation.
* **A slide-by-slide storyboard, listing for each slide:**
  * Slide number
  * Section it belongs to
  * **Assertion headline:** One sentence, a specific arguable claim, not a topic label.
  * **Visual type:** Be precise (e.g., *"grouped bar chart comparing willingness-to-pay across three customer segments,"* not just *"bar chart"*).
  * One sentence explaining what the audience should believe after this slide *(distinct from the headline, which states the claim; this sentence describes the effect on the audience's understanding)*.

### Storyboard Rules & Parameters:
* Your storyboard should contain between **7 and 9 slides**. An executive summary counts as one slide. 
* Appendix slides are not listed in the storyboard but should be noted as a group with a brief description of what they contain and why they were moved out of the body.

### Final Compliance Check:
* Can someone read only the assertion headlines in sequence and follow your argument from context to recommendation without any visuals? 
* Does your deck engage the strongest alternative business model and explain, with evidence, why you are not recommending it?
* Is every visual type chosen because it makes the message clearer, not because it fills space?
* Does your SCQA complication directly motivate the specific question your deck answers? 
* Does your executive summary slide, read alone, give the panel enough to understand and evaluate your recommendation without the rest of the deck?

**Format:** Two pages maximum. The storyboard can be structured as a Markdown table. Begin building actual slides immediately after submission; Week 6 is the pitch.
