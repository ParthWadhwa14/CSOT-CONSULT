**Total estimated time:** ~8–10 hours across 5 days. Concepts build on each other; do them in order.

## Day 1: What Consultants Actually Do When They "Structure" a Problem
**Estimated time:** ~1.5 hours

Most people think problem-solving means jumping to solutions. Consultants start earlier, by questioning whether they're solving the right problem in the first place. Day 1 establishes this mindset shift before introducing any specific tool.

### Read first:
1. **"How Top-Tier Consultants at McKinsey, Bain, and BCG Really Solve Problems"**
   * **Source:** StrategyU
   * **Overview:** A short, well-written piece by a former McKinsey consultant on the core mindset difference between regular problem-solving and structured consulting thinking. Explains why consultants call their meetings "problem-solving sessions" and what that means in practice.
   * **Link:** https://strategyu.co/problem-solving-101/
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

2. **"The BCG and McKinsey Problem Solving Process - A Step-by-Step Guide."**
   * **Source:** Slideworks
   * **Overview:** Covers the hypothesis-led approach that BCG and McKinsey use from Day 1 on any engagement. Good overview of the full arc, from problem definition to structured analysis to recommendations. Read this to understand how the whole process hangs together before you learn the individual tools.
   * **Link:** https://slideworks.io/resources/mckinsey-problem-solving-process
   * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

### Then watch:
* **Video 1: "How to Approach Problems Like a Management Consultant (shortened webinar)"**
  * **Source:** Emory University's Goizueta Business School (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** A business school webinar covering how consultants approach and frame problems, with an academic rather than case-interview lens. Good first watch precisely because it establishes the mindset before any specific tools are introduced.
  * **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uch9ISq9rgE
  * **Estimated time:** 25 minutes

## Day 2: The MECE Principle
**Estimated time:** ~2 hours

MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) is the foundation of all structured problem-solving in consulting. Every issue tree, every framework, every slide deck is evaluated against it. You need to understand it deeply, not just be able to define it.

### Read first:
3. **"The MECE Principle: Definition and Examples."**
   * **Source:** Career in Consulting
   * **Overview:** Covers the definition, how to test whether a structure is MECE, and the two questions you ask yourself to check: "Are there any other conditions that must be true?" and "Can you change one issue independently of the others?" Includes worked examples.
   * **Link:** https://careerinconsulting.com/mece-principle/
   * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

4. **"MECE Framework Explained with Principles and Examples."**
   * **Source:** CaseBasix
   * **Overview:** Supplements the above with more examples across different problem types. Pay attention to the section on common MECE pitfalls - overlapping categories and missing elements are the two mistakes almost everyone makes the first time.
   * **Link:** https://www.casebasix.com/pages/mece-framework
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

### Then watch:
* **Video 2: "Learn MECE in 8 Minutes | 5 Easy Ways to be MECE"**
  * **Source:** Hacking the Case Interview (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** An 8-minute video that goes beyond the definition and shows five concrete ways to apply MECE when decomposing a problem. More practical than Video 2 and directly sets up Day 3's content on issue trees - the five MECE structures covered here are the same building blocks used to construct branches in an issue tree.
  * **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=baAuw5XoHY8
  * **Estimated time:** 8 minutes 

* **Video 3: "How to Structure Your Thoughts Like a Consultant (MECE Principles Explained)"**
  * **Source:** YouTube
  * **Overview:** Covers the MECE principle specifically through the lens of structuring your thinking, not just defining the concept, which makes it a natural bridge from Day 2's definition-focused reading into Day 3's work on issue trees.
  * **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GBBUmRGJk0g
  * **Estimated time:** 5 minutes.

---

> ### Day 2 Exercise
> Take this statement: *"A company's revenue is declining. The possible causes are: fewer customers, lower prices, and problems with the sales team."*
> 
> **Is this MECE? Why or why not? Rewrite it so it is.**
> 
> *(Answer: "Sales team" overlaps with "fewer customers" - sales team issues cause fewer customers. A MECE decomposition would be: Revenue = Volume × Price, then decompose Volume into customer count and purchase frequency, and Price into list price and discounts/mix. Sales team sits under volume drivers - not a separate branch.)*

## Day 3: Issue Trees and Hypothesis Trees
**Estimated time:** ~2.5 hours

Issue trees are the primary tool consultants use to organize a problem before touching any data. A hypothesis tree is a more advanced version where every branch is a falsifiable hypothesis, not just a topic. Day 3 covers both - understand the difference.

### Read first:
5. **"Issue Trees: The Definitive Guide."**
   * **Source:** Crafting Cases
   * **Overview:** The most thorough free guide on issue trees available online. Covers what they are, the difference between diagnostic trees (finding a root cause) and solution trees (finding the best option), how to make branches MECE, and how to prioritize which branch to investigate first. Read all the way through, the examples in the middle are the most useful part.
   * **Link:** https://www.craftingcases.com/issue-tree-guide/
   * **Estimated time:** 30 minutes

6. **"Issue Tree Guide with Examples and Templates."**
   * **Source:** CaseBasix
   * **Overview:** Shorter and more visual than the above. Good as a second read to consolidate the concepts with a different set of examples. Pay attention to the section on how issue trees are used in real consulting engagements—not just in case interviews.
   * **Link:** https://www.casebasix.com/pages/issue-trees
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

7. **"How McKinsey Uses Hypotheses in Business and Strategy."**
   * **Source:** Stratechi
   * **Overview:** Explains the difference between being "gut-driven" and being "hypothesis-driven." This is the most important conceptual read of the week. A hypothesis is not a guess - it is a specific, falsifiable statement that can be proven or disproven with data. The article explains exactly how consultants form, test, and update hypotheses.
   * **Link:** https://www.stratechi.com/hypotheses/
   * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

### Then watch:
* **Video 4: "Learn Issue Trees for Case Interviews in 8 Minutes"**
  * **Source:** Hacking the Case Interview (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** Despite the case interview framing, this is a clean, efficient walkthrough of what an issue tree looks like in practice. Watch it with a pen and try to draw along.
  * **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UZivOkyCa7Y
  * **Estimated time:** 8 minutes

* **Video 5: "The McKinsey Issue Tree Explained: How to Solve Any Complex Problem."**
  * **Source:** YouTube (published 2025)
  * **Overview:** A longer, more detailed video (~20 minutes) that covers both issue trees and hypothesis trees with worked examples. This is the best single video on the topic. Watch it carefully and pause to draw out the trees yourself as the presenter builds them.
  * **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aEQ90SjYOlo
  * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

* **Video 6: "Issue Tree Example (Unusual Case Interview Problem)."**
  * **Source:** PrepLounge (YouTube)
  * **Overview:** A worked example of an issue tree being built in real time for a non-standard problem. Watching someone build a tree from scratch, including their false starts and corrections, is more instructive than seeing a finished, polished example.
  * **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_-ZQkVuAhCE
  * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

## Day 4: Problem Definition and Scoping
**Estimated time:** ~2 hours

Structuring a problem well requires first ensuring you're solving the right one. This is harder than it sounds. Day 4 covers how to write a proper problem statement, how to scope what's in and out, and the common mistake of jumping to structure before properly defining the question.

### Read first:
8. **"The McKinsey Approach to Problem Solving."**
   * **Source:** Umbrex
   * **Overview:** Covers the problem statement framework used at McKinsey: context, objective, scope, constraints, and stakeholders. The section on "scope vs. constraints" is particularly useful: scope is about which markets or segments are included; constraints are about which solutions are on or off the table. A structured problem statement removes ambiguity before any analysis begins.
   * **Link:** https://umbrex.com/resources/mckinsey-problem-solving/
   * **Estimated time:** 20 minutes

9. **"Hypothesis-Driven Approach: The Definitive Guide."**
   * **Source:** Career in Consulting
   * **Overview:** Explains the difference between bottom-up (data first, then conclusion) and top-down (hypothesis first, then test) problem-solving, and walks through the concrete cycle consultants use: form a hypothesis, craft an issue tree around what must be true for the hypothesis to hold, gather data to validate or refute it, then iterate. Well-structured and written for someone learning the concept for the first time rather than preparing for a case interview.
   * **Link:** https://careerinconsulting.com/hypothesis/
   * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes 

### Then watch:
* **Video 7: "Hypothesis-Driven Approach - How Consultants Use It"**
  * **Source:** YouTube
  * **Overview:** A clean video explaining the hypothesis-driven approach with a real consulting context. Covers how forming a hypothesis early focuses the work, why it's not guessing, and how to update your hypothesis without losing structure.
  * **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=43tJxTFB7xU
  * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

## Day 5: Applying the Week to the Problem Statement
**Estimated time:** ~1.5 hours

Day 5 has no new concepts. The tools are all in place. This day is about turning them into a real, ambiguous, open-ended business problem - the Sarvam AI problem statement you have been given separately. Read the problem statement carefully before starting Day 5.

### Read:
10. **"Issue Tree - Caseinterview.com"**
    * **Overview:** More concise than the Day 3 reads, but with a useful framing: the issue tree is not just a tool for analysis, it is an argument. Each branch, if true, contributes to the overall answer. Read with that lens before you attempt to build one for Sarvam.
    * **Link:** https://caseinterview.com/issue-tree
    * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes

11. **"How to Use the Issue Tree in Case Interviews."**
    * **Source:** PrepLounge
    * **Overview:** Covers the answer-first / results-first orientation and the Pareto principle in consulting - you don't need to investigate every branch equally; you prioritize based on where the biggest impact is most likely. This is a practical point that separates good issue trees from great ones.
    * **Link:** https://www.preplounge.com/en/bootcamp.php/case-cracking-toolbox/structure-your-thoughts/issue-tree
    * **Estimated time:** 15 minutes.

### Guided warm-up (not submitted - 20 minutes):
Before building your tree for Sarvam, do this first. Re-read the problem statement and answer these three questions in writing:
1. **What is the actual decision being made?** Write it as a single, specific question - not "what should Sarvam do" but the precise choice the founders need to make.
2. **What would make one business model clearly better than another?** List 3–4 dimensions you would need to evaluate - for example, time to revenue, or competitive defensibility. These dimensions, not the business models themselves, should become your top-level branches.
3. **What is your opening hypothesis?** State it as a specific, falsifiable claim - not "Sarvam should focus on enterprise," but *"Sarvam's strongest path to commercial sustainability is B2B enterprise voice agents because Indian enterprises have demonstrated willingness to pay for vernacular customer service automation and no well-funded competitor owns that space yet."* You will test this hypothesis with data over the coming weeks, not defend it now.

*This warm-up is the thinking that goes into your tree. Do not skip it and jump straight to drawing branches.*

## Task Week 1 Deliverable (submitted for evaluation)
Using the Sarvam AI problem statement and the tools from this week, produce a **one-page issue tree**. This is your Week 1 assessed submission.

### Your submission must include, on a single page with no prose paragraphs:
* **A clearly stated central question:** One sentence, specific and answerable.
* **At least 3 MECE top-level branches:** These must be dimensions of the decision, not the business model options themselves.
* **2–3 sub-questions under each branch:** Each one a specific question that, if answered, would help resolve that branch.
* **One falsifiable hypothesis per top-level branch:** A specific claim that can be proven or disproven with data.

### Before submitting, check your tree against these questions:
* Can any sub-question sit under more than one branch? If so, your branches overlap—fix them.
* Is there a dimension of the decision that has no branch at all? If so, your tree is not exhaustive—add it.
* Does each hypothesis name a specific condition, not just restate the branch label? *"The enterprise market is large"* is not a hypothesis. *"Indian enterprises in BFSI and telecom will pay ₹15–40 lakhs annually for a multilingual voice agent that reduces call centre costs by 30%"* is.
* Could someone who hasn't read the problem statement understand what decision this tree is helping to make, just from the central question and branch labels?

**Format:** One page (Clarity of logic matters more than visual polish). Submit within week 1.
